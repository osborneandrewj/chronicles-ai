# v0.6.2 Milestone

**Target**: TBD
**Bar**: "The narrator shows instead of tells — never narrates other characters' private history or the protagonist's interior motives as fact; the player's typed action is legible in the first paragraph; supernatural foreshadowing is rationed instead of drumbeat; repeated pet phrases and stock images stop echoing turn after turn; the prose voice is grounded, sensory, and built from Stephen King's *On Writing* craft principles rather than style mimicry."

v0.6.1 ([`docs/21-v0.6.1-milestone.md`](21-v0.6.1-milestone.md)) tightened narrator prose against fourth-wall breaks, DM voice, and citation tics. v0.6.2 is a follow-on style pass: it addresses problems that the v0.6.1 rules don't name, surfaced by a long play session on 2026-05-25 (Spokane-suburbia world, first-day-of-something premise). Mobile + audio + replay-cost work was pushed back from this scope; v0.6.3 ([`docs/22-v0.6.3-milestone.md`](22-v0.6.3-milestone.md)) became the Safari audio fix, v0.6.4 ([`docs/25-v0.6.4-milestone.md`](25-v0.6.4-milestone.md)) is story-dossier memory, v0.6.5 ([`docs/26-v0.6.5-milestone.md`](26-v0.6.5-milestone.md)) is mobile + replay-cost, and archivist canonicalization is v0.6.6 ([`docs/27-v0.6.6-milestone.md`](27-v0.6.6-milestone.md)).

## Why

Five concrete diagnoses from the 2026-05-25 session, each demonstrated by quoted output or repeated pattern:

1. **The narrator tells the player things the player wouldn't know — about other characters.** Example (Marcus, after the player walks past him): *"He's learned, over fourteen months, that this is what you look like when you already have the answer."* The protagonist cannot see that Marcus has "learned" anything; this is narrator-side character analysis stated as fact. Same shape: *"Joseph has always been good for this — the Marine in him goes straight to logistics, no sentimentality, no inflation."* Both are exposition dressed as observation.
2. **The narrator tells the player their own motives as fact.** Example (banking-app scene): *"You opened it because the eagle is still behind your eyes and the deleted account is still in your memory and ancient vast presences that manifest in suburban SUVs are harder to sit with than a balance sheet."* The narrator should never speak the protagonist's internal causality as established. The player typed *"I open my banking app and look at it as I walk"* — interpreting *why* belongs to the player.
3. **Foreshadowing is drumbeat, not weather.** Every turn carries a supernatural portent — "the pressure behind your sternum", "patient as geology", "the cedar… too still", "scales", "an eagle that is not quite a sun". Pancakes and balcony coffee both end on cosmic-arrival beats. The effect inverts: when the actual supernatural event lands, it isn't elevated because the prose has been at that pitch all morning. Foreshadowing needs to be rationed — most mundane turns should be allowed to be mundane.
4. **The player's typed action is not reflected in the opening of the narration.** Player typed *"I get out of bed, and go to the kitchen to make pancakes for my children, James (18), Desiree (12), Jacqueline (10), Carlie (6)."* Narrator opened with *"The third floorboard announces you before you even reach it."* The names and ages never land naturally. A later reader of the transcript can't see what the player chose — it's hidden in the prompt-only history. The narration should make the player's decision legible without forcing it.
5. **The narrator repeats pet phrases and stock images across adjacent turns.** "Pressure behind/in your chest" and sky-colour observations can appear four or five times in a row, especially when the model is reaching for continuity or mood. This turns motifs into verbal tics. Recurrence is allowed only when it changes, escalates, resolves, or is noticed by the protagonist; otherwise the narrator should find the next concrete thing in the room.

The cumulative effect: the prose is competent but doesn't trust the reader, doesn't trust the scene, and doesn't trust the player.

The intended craft anchor, named by the user: **Stephen King at his most domestic, read through *On Writing*** — sensory, physical, present-tense observation that earns its weight before going strange. The useful signal is not "imitate King's sentences"; it is King's craft advice applied to an interactive narrator: use concrete nouns and active verbs, trust the reader to infer motive, let description begin on the page and finish in the reader's imagination, keep dialogue attribution and surrounding business clean, and revise away explanation that only tells the reader what the scene already shows. King doesn't need to tell you what the husband across the kitchen has learned over fourteen years; he can show the husband not looking up, and let you do the work.

One workstream. Prompt-only in the minimal version; a small code surface only if the prompt-only rewrite doesn't move the needle.

```

                                git-diffable as markdown, matching CLAUDE.md and

                                or otherwise preserve existing imports with the
                                smallest possible shim.

    New rule sections to add / strengthen:
    - "Show, don't tell" — explicit forbidden patterns:
        * Narrating any other character's private history, learned behaviour,
          internal state, or back-history as fact ("he's learned over X
          years…", "the Marine in him goes straight to logistics", "she
          remembers when she was…"). Render only what the protagonist could
          observe: posture, choice of word, what they did or didn't do.
        * Narrating the protagonist's interior causality as fact ("you opened
          the app because…", "you texted him instead of thinking about X",
          "you knew Kyle was wrong because…"). State action and sensation;
          let motive be inferable, not declared.
        * Explaining causation between observed events from outside the
          protagonist's frame ("Marcus stopped looking, which means X is
          working").
    - "Foreshadowing is weather, not drumbeat" — rationing rule:
        * Most turns should not carry a supernatural / portent beat at all.
          Mundane stances (do/say/observe with no escalating state) lean
          ordinary; the strange enters when the state or scene demands it,
          not as ambient atmosphere.
        * When foreshadowing lands, one image per turn is enough — not three
          rolled together (pressure + cedar + scales in the same paragraph).
        * Heuristic: if the protagonist's action is domestic, mechanical, or
          social-routine, the narration should be domestic, mechanical, or
          social-routine. Strange weather comes when the scene shifts gear.
    - "No pet phrase loops" — repetition control:
        * Do not repeat the same distinctive phrase, body sensation, weather
          image, sky colour, or portent object in adjacent turns unless it has
          changed or the protagonist actively attends to it.
        * A motif may return, but it must do new work: escalate, recede,
          contradict earlier perception, trigger a choice, or become materially
          relevant. Otherwise move to a fresh concrete observation.
        * Treat repeated phrases like "pressure in your chest", "the sky is
          [colour]", "patient as geology", "too still", and "ancient/vast
          presence" as spent after one use until the scene changes.
    - "Open on the player's move" — opening-paragraph rule:
        * The first paragraph of any non-opening turn must make the player's
          concrete action or speech legible in-scene, so a later reader of the
          transcript can see what the player chose without the prompt. Do not
          mechanically restate the player's sentence; dramatize the move.
        * Named characters / ages / specific objects in the player's input
          surface in the opening, not pages later. "Pancakes for the kids"
          → the names appear in the first or second beat, not "Carlie is
          already at the kitchen table" with no mention that the player
          named all four.
    - "Craft anchor from On Writing" — technique, not pastiche:
        * Stephen King at his most domestic — sensory, physical, present-tense
          observation; trust the reader; let mundane be mundane until it
          isn't. The supernatural is more frightening when it interrupts the
          ordinary than when it permeates it.
        * Prefer concrete nouns and active verbs over explanatory abstraction.
          Cut adverbial explanation around speech and action when the action can
          carry the meaning.
        * Description should give the reader a few sharp, ordinary particulars,
          not a full inventory. Leave room for the reader to complete the room.
        * Dialogue should be carried by what characters say, what they do while
          saying it, and what the protagonist can observe — not by narrator
          labels explaining emotion, motive, or subtext.
    - "Contrastive examples" — add four compact bad -> better pairs:
        * Other-character private history as fact -> observable behavior.
        * Protagonist motive as fact -> action + sensation, motive inferable.
        * Supernatural drumbeat -> ordinary domestic beat unless scene state
          earns one strange image.
        * Repeated stock phrase -> fresh concrete beat or changed motif.

B. Code-side reinforcement (only if A alone doesn't move the needle)
  src/lib/classifier.ts         EDIT?  Add a small signal to the narrator
                                context: "previous N turns contained
                                foreshadowing image: true/false". Cheap
                                heuristic (regex on a small image set, or a
                                Haiku call). Gives the prompt something to
                                bind to ("if recent_foreshadowing == true,
                                stay ordinary").
  src/lib/prompt.ts             EDIT. Wire the signal into the narrator's
                                trailing block.
```

### Stephen King / *On Writing* craft notes to encode

These are craft principles, not an instruction to imitate King's copyrighted prose:

- **Concrete beats beat explanation.** Prefer the object, gesture, sound, smell,
  or pressure the protagonist can perceive over narrator-side diagnosis.
- **Active verbs carry more weight than modifiers.** If a sentence needs an
  adverb to explain a line of dialogue or an action, rewrite the action so the
  meaning is visible.
- **Description is selective.** Give a few sharp particulars and let the reader
  complete the room; don't inventory every object or explain the symbolic weight
  of the image.
- **Trust the reader.** Motive, subtext, and dread should be inferable from
  behavior, timing, omission, and sensory pressure. The narrator should not
  underline the lesson after every beat.
- **Motifs are not tics.** A repeated image should return only because it has
  changed, paid off, or become actionable. If the sky, chest pressure, or a
  strange stillness has already carried the previous beat, the next beat should
  look elsewhere.
- **Situation before thesis.** Start from the player's concrete move and the
  present scene pressure. Let plot and theme emerge from what happens, not from
  summary or authorial commentary.
- **Revision cuts explanation.** In the prompt rewrite, every new rule should
  help the narrator remove an unnecessary tell, not add a new kind of lecture.

Worked examples for the prompt should be this compact:

```
Bad: Marcus has learned, over fourteen months, that this is what you look like when you already have the answer.
Better: Marcus looks once at your face, then down at the mug between his hands. "You already know," he says.

Bad: You open the banking app because the eagle is still behind your eyes.
Better: You thumb open the banking app. The balance loads in clean black numbers while the crosswalk ticks beside you.

Bad: The pancakes hiss in the pan, and behind the cedar the ancient thing waits, vast and patient.
Better: The pancakes brown too fast at the edges. Carlie asks for the small plate with the blue chip in it.

Bad: The pressure in your chest tightens. Outside, the sky has gone the colour of old bone.
Bad again: The pressure in your chest sits there as the bone-coloured sky presses lower.
Better: You rinse batter from the fork. The water runs warm over your knuckles, and James says, "Mom, the car's blocking the driveway."
```

## Open questions

- **Foreshadowing — what's the protagonist's baseline psychological state?** The 2026-05-25 session's protagonist has cosmic-pressure-behind-the-sternum from turn one because the premise calls for it. A truly mundane premise should not have that pressure at all. The rule should distinguish "ambient dread the premise installed" from "narrator reaching for atmosphere unprompted." Worth a worked example.
- **Reference author footprint inside the prompt.** The prompt should name *On Writing* as a craft anchor only if the surrounding text explicitly says "technique, not pastiche." If play-test output starts sounding like horror-brand mimicry instead of clean domestic observation, remove the name and keep the craft principles.
- **Do we need a recent-image tracker?** Prompt-only may be enough for repeated phrase/image loops because the last ~20 turns are already in narrator context. If repetition persists after A, the B signal could track both recent foreshadowing and recent stock images rather than adding two separate mechanisms.
- **Code signal for foreshadowing rationing.** If A misses, the B fork (recent-foreshadowing boolean) needs a detector. Regex on a small image set ("pressure", "patient", "ancient", "waiting", "vast") is cheap and brittle. A Haiku call is precise but adds latency and cost to every turn. Defer the decision until A's result is in.

## Explicit cuts

- **Per-character prose voice.** v0.7 territory; needs archivist support.
- **Per-character TTS voices.** v0.7; already parked.
- **Narrator length re-tuning.** Length rules stay as-is; show-don't-tell will pull length down naturally.
- **Dialogue formatting changes.** Italic for texts and inline attribution stay. Only the surrounding narration changes.
- **POV / tense changes.** Second-person present is correct.
- **Code-signal foreshadowing detector (B).** Pulled from v0.6.2 unless A demonstrably misses.
- **Dedicated narrative-style state file.** Out unless repetition persists after
  the prompt move. The prompt itself should carry runtime style rules; a
  separate human-facing style guide can wait until the style surface grows.
- **Tests beyond manual evaluation.** No automated narrator-output tests in v0.6.2.

## Accepted tradeoffs

- **Manual qualitative evaluation is high-effort and low-coverage.** Two play sessions catch obvious regressions but won't surface edge cases. Acceptable because the alternative — narrator output assertions — is either trivially brittle (regex on exact strings) or prohibitively expensive (LLM-judge per turn). Revisit if a third style pass becomes necessary.
- **The rewrite may regress some v0.6.1 wins.** Tightening "show, don't tell" risks losing the diegetic-recall patterns v0.6.1 added ("the name surfaces again — Wilkes, hands flat on the table"). The new rules need to forbid *external* narrator analysis of other characters' history without forbidding the protagonist's *own* in-scene remembering. The contrastive examples must make this distinction.
- **Reduced foreshadowing risks blandness on horror-premise worlds.** A world whose premise is "something is hunting you" needs ambient dread. The rule "weather, not drumbeat" should be premise-aware; the prompt should not flatten genre. Open question: does the premise text need a tone/intensity tag to bias the narrator? Defer.
- **Author name in the prompt is a known LLM-quirk surface.** Naming Stephen King may produce mimicry that's the wrong King. Mitigated by tying the reference to *On Writing* craft principles, explicitly forbidding pastiche, and evaluating the first play session for horror-cadence drift.
- **Prompt-only repetition control may be leaky.** The model can still reach for a phrase it used two turns ago, especially under horror pressure. Acceptable for A because recent history is already in context; if the smoke test still shows phrase loops, promote B into a small "recent images used" signal.
- **Prompt migration to `prompts/narrator-system.md` bundled with rewrite.** One PR doing two things. Tolerable because both touch the same prompt content; splitting would mean rewriting against the JS template literal first, then moving, then re-diffing.

## Scope expansion during implementation (2026-05-25)

Implementation began as the prompt-only narrator-style rewrite scoped above. During testing on world "2026" (Spokane premise, pulled from prod for evaluation), additional architectural work was scoped in and shipped as part of v0.6.2 rather than deferred. The original narrator-style work remains intact — these are additions, not replacements.

### What landed beyond the original scope

- **Per-character observation memory** (`characters.observations TEXT`, append-only with `[t:N]` provenance). NPCs accumulate observations of the protagonist's behavior. The archivist learns to record deviations ("noticed Andrew repeated the same line three times"); the narrator reads them and lets NPCs act on what they've noticed. Migration v7.
- **Agentic NPCs — full pipeline.** Two tiers on `characters.agency_level` (`npc` default, `agent` promoted). New `personal_goals`, `current_focus`, `recent_activity`, `appearance_count` fields. New NPC agent (Haiku-based, `src/lib/npc-agent.ts` + `prompts/npc-agent-system.md`) that runs **before** the narrator each turn, parallel with the classifier. The agent emits two outputs:
  - `npc_updates` — state changes reflecting what each NPC experienced in the prior turn (focus shifts, off-scene activity log entries, optional location moves, rare personal-goals updates)
  - `planned_actions` — per-present-agent intents for the upcoming turn that the narrator stages as the actual scene
  
  Migration v8. Cost: one batched Haiku call per turn when any agent-tier NPCs exist; zero overhead otherwise.
- **Auto-promotion at appearance threshold.** Deterministic (no LLM) — `src/lib/npc-promotion.ts` bumps `appearance_count` for present NPCs each turn and flips `npc → agent` at threshold 3. Newly-promoted NPCs get their first agentic plan on the same turn they cross.
- **Narrator-prompt extensions** to honor the new state:
  - "The world has its own pulse" — narrator may advance the clock, let NPCs initiate, and bring premise pressure in on small player moves rather than waiting for the player
  - Honor `personal goal` / `focus` / `activity` / `observed` lines in state — NPCs have their own lives, the protagonist is not the centre of every NPC's attention
  - Honor `PLANNED MOVES THIS TURN` — agent NPCs' decisions are commitments, not suggestions; narrator stages them
  - "State-block fields are inputs, not exports" — explicit rule that everything in the state block is *the writer's notes*, never to be narrated as interior fact ("Marcus has decided", "fear wearing a controlled mask")
  - "Repetition in the fiction is a signal" — when the protagonist repeats themselves, narrator must let NPCs notice and the scene architecture must break by the third pass
  - "Open on the player's move" — for `say` stance especially, the protagonist's line must appear on the page early so a future book reader (turns will compile into a printable book) can reconstruct what was said
  - "Never narrate about your own mechanics" — explicit ban on "premise block noted", "your next turn", "the fiction is running" 4th-wall failure mode surfaced in play
- **Archivist character rule broadened.** Now covers three categories explicitly: present-and-named, present-but-unnamed-if-distinct-enough-to-recur (give a descriptive identifier), and mentioned-but-off-scene (real characters referenced in dialogue but not in the immediate scene). Closes the gap where Mike / Diane / Ricky / the Man in the High-Vis Vest weren't being persisted.
- **Inspector updates** (`src/components/WorldInspector.tsx`) — surfaces `agent` badge, `personal goals`, `focus`, `activity`, `observed`, all with tinted labels.
- **Cost UI** — new `npc N in / M out` segment in the per-turn cost footer; `npc_agent` rolled into the daily token cap query (`src/lib/cost-cap.ts`) and per-world `UsageTotals`.
- **Graceful degradation** — NPC agent failures are caught pre-narrator and never block the stream; turn metadata captures the error for debugging while the narrator runs plan-less.
