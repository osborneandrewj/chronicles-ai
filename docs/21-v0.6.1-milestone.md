# v0.6.1 Milestone

**Target**: TBD
**Bar**: "Narrator prose reads the way Andrew wants it to read; a new world begins with the narrator making the first move; the narrator never breaks the fourth wall and absorbs small player-introduced details into the fiction; NPCs create believable scene pressure and feel like people with partial knowledge, uneven intelligence, private incentives, and agency; the obvious post-v0.6.0 sharp edges (cost-cap drift, TTS cross-world write, detached archivist, unbounded history load) are gone."
**Budget**: TBD — two workstreams; size after dynamic prose + NPC-goal migration is estimated.

v0.6.0 shipped the typed-world-state pipeline on 2026-05-25 ([`docs/20-v0.6.0-milestone.md`](20-v0.6.0-milestone.md)). v0.6.1 is a follow-on patch: tighten the seams that v0.6.0 exposed during the audit pass, address narrator prose feedback from real play, and give NPCs immediate goals and bounded cognition so scenes stop stalling. Mobile UI is split out into [`docs/22-v0.6.2-milestone.md`](22-v0.6.2-milestone.md). No new tables, no new agents; a small `characters` schema extension for NPC goals is allowed.

## Why

Four motivating problems, in priority order:

1. **Narrator prose needs tuning.** The narrator currently feels stiff and directionless. It is too verbose at the wrong times: 2-3 short paragraphs can work when entering a bar or crossing into a new scene, but the same length is deadening when the player simply says hi to the barman. New worlds also need an opening move from the narrator so the player starts inside a live situation instead of staring at an empty prompt. Response length needs to follow the fiction and the classified move.
2. **The narrator breaks the fourth wall to refuse player-introduced details.** Observed in v0.6.0 smoke play: the player tried to introduce a Vox Skull descending from their lander (a genre-consistent Inquisitor companion in a 40k setting), and the narrator replied *"The authoritative state lists no vox-skull among your equipment..."* That's an out-of-character refusal that quotes the state block at the player. Root cause: `world-state.ts:93` tells the narrator "do not contradict... narrate the attempt" but doesn't distinguish **fixed facts** (you're alone on the pad) from **open canvas** (what's in your pack is unspecified, so a small fiction-consistent item is fair game). The narrator should weave small player additions into the fiction and deflect power-shifting ones *in-fiction* — never with an OOC refusal.
3. **v0.6.0 left small, real bugs that aren't visible until they bite.** The daily cost-cap silently undercounts current archivist usage, TTS char accounting isn't world-scoped, `/api/chat` will 500 on malformed JSON, and the archivist patch runs as a detached promise that can lose work across deploys.
4. **Scenes can stall because NPCs are passive or too transparently assistant-like.** NPCs should have immediate, dynamic goals and believable bounded cognition so they can create pressure, offers, evasions, interruptions, demands, mistakes, and consequences without hijacking the player character. The target is not "quest-giver with better prose"; it is "person in the world with partial knowledge, uneven intelligence, private incentives, and agency."

## Narrator + Player Additions + NPC Goals Direction

The narrator should adapt its output length to the player's move instead of always aiming for a fixed 2-4 paragraph response.

Good target behaviour:

- **Opening a new world:** the narrator makes the first move. Use 2-3 short paragraphs to place the protagonist in a concrete moment, show the immediate sensory texture, introduce one live pressure or invitation, and leave the player with an obvious thing they can do. The opening should be in-fiction, not a system explanation or premise questionnaire.
- **Entering / changing a scene:** 2-3 short paragraphs is fine. Establish the room, the sensory impression, who is present, and one live tension.
- **Small dialogue beat:** usually 1 short paragraph. Let the NPC answer or react; do not pad with atmosphere unless the reaction changes the situation.
- **Observation:** give the useful sensory facts cleanly. Do not advance the scene clock or inject a new event unless the state/history already points there.
- **Thinking:** stay inside the protagonist's perception. Keep it brief and do not move external reality.
- **High-consequence action / danger / discovery:** expand as needed, but earn the length with consequences.

The narrator should also stop refusing player-introduced details out-of-character. The smoke-test failure was the narrator replying *"The authoritative state lists no vox-skull..."* — quoting the system at the player. New posture:

- **Never break the fourth wall.** No references to "the state", "what is listed", "the system", the narrator's role, or the player as a player.
- **Distinguish FIXED from OPEN.** Place, present characters, time, established events are fixed and shouldn't be silently overwritten. Equipment, off-scene history, small companions, and unspecified background detail are open canvas — the player can paint into them.
- **Absorb the small.** A vox-skull on an Inquisitor, a flask, a familiar, a habit, a worn item that fits the genre and role — weave it in and let the archivist canonize it.
- **Deflect the large in-fiction.** A titan, a god-weapon, an army at your back, a retcon of an established fact — the device sputters, the holster is empty, the figure was never there. Always inside the story.
- **Examples of acceptable additions (Inquisitor on an Imperial world):** vox-skull, dataslate, autopistol, sanctified blade, servo-skull familiar, signet ring, rosarius.
- **Examples that warrant in-fiction deflection:** an entire retinue when you arrived alone, a Land Raider in your jacket pocket, a saint's relic that wasn't established, a second player character.

NPCs should no longer be frozen props. Present NPCs need lightweight, dynamic goals that let scenes move under their own pressure:

- Goals should be immediate and playable, not sprawling plot outlines.
- Examples: "get the room closed before the constable arrives", "avoid revealing what she saw", "sell the player a room", "find out why the player is asking about the wreck".
- NPCs can act on goals through pressure, offers, interruptions, evasions, demands, lies, exits, and consequences.
- NPCs must not force the player character's decisions. They can change the situation around the player; they cannot choose the player's response.
- Not every NPC should be overactive. The narrator should use goals when fictionally appropriate, especially when the scene would otherwise idle.
- The archivist should update goals only when the latest turn clearly changes them. Omitted goal means "unchanged", not "clear it".

NPC intelligence should come from **private state + bounded reasoning + social strategy**, not just from more dramatic dialogue. The v0.6.1 implementation can stay small, but the narrator prompt should treat each present NPC as having a tiny implied mind:

- **Immediate motive:** what the NPC wants right now.
- **Partial knowledge:** what the NPC knows, suspects, misunderstands, or does not know.
- **Risk tolerance:** bold, cautious, reckless, desperate, resigned, dutiful.
- **Social strategy:** charm, intimidation, evasion, bargaining, deference, sarcasm, silence, distraction.
- **Competence band:** brilliant, average, naive, foolish, drunk, distracted, traumatized, domain-expert, socially sharp, technically ignorant.

These do not all need to be schema fields in v0.6.1. `active_goal` is the state hook; `memorable_facts` can carry stable traits; the narrator can infer temporary posture from the scene. If a second small column is approved, `current_attitude TEXT` is the best companion to `active_goal` because it shapes how a goal is pursued without requiring a full mind model.

Good target behaviour:

- NPCs are not quest terminals. They act from partial knowledge, social position, appetite, fear, pride, fatigue, loyalty, status, and immediate incentives.
- Smart NPCs should not all sound eloquent, calm, or correct. Intelligence should appear through what they notice, conceal, infer, ask, withhold, exploit, and choose not to say.
- Intelligence is uneven and domain-specific. A smuggler can be socially brilliant and legally ignorant; a noble savant can be strategically terrifying and emotionally childish; a guard can be dull in conversation but excellent at noticing shoes, fear, and forged papers.
- Dumb NPCs still have agency. They may simplify, overreact, trust the wrong authority, repeat rumors, miss subtle threats, double down when embarrassed, or accidentally reveal important information.
- NPCs may be mistaken, evasive, petty, curious, distracted, suspicious, proud, merciful, bored, or impulsive. They should not optimize for helping the player unless helping the player serves their own motives.
- NPCs can lie, stall, probe, counter-question, leave, interrupt, make offers, call for help, destroy evidence, shift prices, warn others, or change their mind. They cannot decide the player character's choices or internal response.
- The narrator should usually show NPC motives through behavior, not exposition. Prefer "The bartender's eyes flick once toward the back door. 'Constable hasn't been here tonight,' he says, too quickly." over "The bartender lies because he is afraid of the constable."
- In scenes with multiple NPCs, they should have different tempos, vocabularies, blind spots, initiative levels, and social priorities. Players should not be able to tell from dialogue alone whether a character is AI-authored or hypothetically human-operated.

## Explicit cuts

- **No new tables.** A single `characters` column for NPC goals is allowed; `current_attitude TEXT` is the only optional second column under consideration. Anything larger waits for v0.7.
- **No full NPC mind schema.** v0.6.1 does not add beliefs, plans, secrets, stats, or social-memory tables. Bounded cognition is prompt/state discipline for now, backed by `active_goal` and existing character facts.
- **No Character Actor / Story Conductor agent.** NPC goals and implied cognition are state hints for the existing narrator, not a new runtime agent loop.
- **No seed-time kit/companions field on world creation.** Considered alongside the in-fiction absorption path; cut to keep scope tight. The absorption path handles the same need without a new form field. Revisit in v0.7 if absorption proves unreliable in practice.
- **No structured items table.** Player-introduced items live in the player character's `memorable_facts`. An items table waits for genuine inventory mechanics.
- **Mobile + desktop UI pass.** Deferred to v0.6.2 ([`docs/22-v0.6.2-milestone.md`](22-v0.6.2-milestone.md)). The biggest TBD in v0.6.1's first draft; pulled out so design work doesn't hold the bug+prose tag.
- **Replay-cost-footer bug ([[v0_6_0_replay_cost_bug]]).** Deferred to v0.6.2. Bundled with the UI workstream since both touch the per-turn footer.
- **Embeddings / `[t:N]` consumer code.** v0.7. The provenance suffix from v0.6.0 keeps accruing; we don't read it yet.
- **CRUD on inspector entities.** v0.7. Still read-only.
- **Per-character voices.** v0.7. `characters.voice_id` continues to sit unused.
- **Postgres migration.** v0.7 or later.
- **Multi-user / per-user world scoping.** Out of scope.
- **Virtualised chat history.** Pagination only; revisit if 60-turn initial load still feels slow on a long world.

## Accepted tradeoffs

- **Two cost-sum SQL strings still drift in principle.** Fixing the immediate drift without consolidating means a v0.7 reader could repeat the mistake. Mitigated by tests in `cost-cap.test.ts` that fail if a known metadata key is unsummed.
- **SIGTERM-await for archivist promises is best-effort.** Railway sends SIGTERM then SIGKILL after a timeout (default ~10s). A patch that takes longer than the grace window still loses. Acceptable because the typical archivist call is <3s and the failure mode is "next turn sees slightly stale state", not data loss.
- **Pagination changes initial-load semantics.** A world with 1000 turns no longer hydrates fully on first paint. The "Load older" button is a UI affordance that didn't exist before; users need to discover it. Accepted because the current behaviour quietly degrades past a few hundred turns and we'd rather show the seam.
- **AbortController on WorldInspector adds one error path** (`AbortError` thrown from `fetch`). Swallow it explicitly to avoid noisy console errors during normal close/reopen.
- **Bundling two workstreams means one of them can hold up the other.** Mitigation: if prose+goals run long, ship bugs as v0.6.1 alone and roll prose+goals into v0.6.2 alongside the UI work.
- **A single active goal per NPC is intentionally reductive.** It may miss richer motivations, but it keeps v0.6.1 small and gives the narrator enough pressure to avoid stalled scenes. v0.7 can grow this into structured goals or a Character Actor if play shows the need. If `current_attitude` lands, it reduces this limitation a little without becoming a full mind model.
- **NPC goals can make the narrator too pushy if overused.** Mitigate in the prompt: goals should create pressure and movement, not seize control or turn every exchange into plot machinery. Human-like NPCs also sometimes stall, evade, misunderstand, wait, or choose self-protection over plot progress.
- **Bounded cognition remains partly implicit.** Without a Character Actor or belief table, the narrator infers partial knowledge, risk tolerance, competence, and social strategy from prose and character facts. This is fuzzier than a structured model, but it preserves v0.6.1's small scope and can be tested through manual play.
- **The narrator's small-vs-power-shifting judgement is fuzzy.** Sometimes it'll absorb something we'd reject (a player conjures a third hand) or deflect something we'd allow (a perfectly ordinary canteen). Cheaper than a structured validator agent and inline with the existing prompt-driven approach. If misjudgements cluster around a recurring shape, tune the prompt; if they're random, accept it.
- **Items live in `memorable_facts`, not a typed inventory.** Means the narrator can't query "do I have a vox-skull" structurally — it reads the facts list. Fine at v0.6.1 scale; v0.7 promotes if needed.

## Exit criteria

1. `npm run lint` and `npm run type-check` pass.
2. `npm test` passes, including the three new test files above.
3. `package.json` reads `0.6.1` on the release branch (bumped on the branch itself per [[feedback_release_version_bump]]).
4. Manual: create world A and world B. Stream a TTS clip on a turn in A. POST `/api/tts/record?worldId=B` with A's `turnId`. Expect 0-row update (verify in SQLite); cost footer for A unchanged.
5. Manual: send `POST /api/chat?worldId=…` with body `not json`. Expect 400, not 500. Repeat with `{}` — expect 400. Repeat with valid body — expect 200 stream.
6. Manual: trigger `SIGTERM` against the dev server mid-stream (kill the process while the archivist is running). Restart. The narrator turn is present; the archivist `updated_at`/`metadata.archivist` is either populated (the await caught it) or absent (timeout). Either is acceptable; what's NOT acceptable is a half-written patch.
7. Manual: open a world with >60 turns. Initial page render shows the latest 60. "Load older" appends the prior batch in order. Scroll position doesn't jump.
8. Manual: rapid-toggle the inspector 5x while a turn is streaming. The final visible state matches `/api/world-state` at the time the toggle settled — no flicker between two stale shapes.
9. Manual (cost-cap regression): seed a turn with `metadata = {"archivist":{"usage":{"inputTokens":150000,"outputTokens":50000}}}` and `created_at = datetime('now')`. `isOverDailyLimit()` returns `true` against the default 200k cap. Before this fix, it returned `false`.
10. Manual (world opening): create a fresh world and land on the play page. Before the player sends any message, the narrator makes the first move: 2-3 short in-fiction paragraphs, concrete location/sensory texture, one live pressure or invitation, and an obvious playable opening. No premise questionnaire, no system explanation, no empty chat.
11. Manual (dynamic prose): play these four turns in a fresh world and compare shape:
    - Enter a bar / tavern / public room → 2-3 short paragraphs, concrete setting, live tension.
    - Say "hi" to the barman → 1 short paragraph, dialogue/reaction, no padded description.
    - Examine the room → concise sensory facts, no major event or time jump.
    - Take a high-consequence action → longer only if consequence warrants it.
12. Manual (NPC goals): create or seed an NPC with an immediate goal. In 3-5 turns where the player hesitates or asks small questions, the NPC should apply believable pressure or pursue the goal without deciding for the player.
13. Manual (NPC bounded cognition): in a scene with 2-3 NPCs, they should show different tempos, vocabularies, blind spots, initiative levels, and social priorities. From dialogue and behavior alone, the player should not be able to tell whether a character is AI-authored or hypothetically human-operated.
